Tong Liu

I'm a PhD candidate in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at New York University, advised by Prof. Zhong-Ping Jiang. I work on distributed optimization, reinforcement learning, systems theory, transportation, and robotics.

I have been pursuing the solutions to the following questions:

  • Break a hard problem into parts—fine. But when do solutions to the parts add up to a solution for the whole?
  • Is it possible to achieve the desired behavior using only coarse information about a system, without detailed knowledge of its internal structure?
Systems theory and machine learning provide principled concepts to address these questions, and I use these ideas throughout my research.

Research Statement

My research focuses on distributed optimization and reinforcement learning for multi-agent systems with applications to transportation and robotics. Throughout my research, all of the effective approaches are particular solutions to the above two questions.

The first question is related to nearly every engineering subject, this is because we often break a complex system into smaller subsystems, each of which achieves a specific goal. This is the case, e.g., to develop a web application, the front end handles user interactions and the back end manages data storage. In distributed optimization, an optimizer reduces the overall loss and an aggregator ensures consensus among agents. In reinforcement learning, an actor learns to improve the policy and a critic evaluates the policy. All of these topics are related to the first question.

In what conditions, can this structure ensure the achievement of the overall goal? Generally speaking, the two modules should be weakly coupled, i.e., the failure of one module does not significantly affect the other module. This idea is related to the concept of the small-gain theorem and time-scale separation in systems theory, which I have applied to robust distributed optimization of complex networks. The result is surprsing: the achievement of the objective can be guaranteed for rather general classes of uncertain systems subject to disturbances, as long as appropriate modules are weakly coupled. Of course, the "weak coupling" condition is non-trivial, and right language should be used to described it.

This is also a potential direction for future research in large language models (LLMs). As the training samples of LLMs are to be run out, how to continue to improve the performance of LLMs will be a challenging problem. To achieve this goal, self-learning can be a promising approach, but how to design the self-learning module and how to ensure the improvement of the overall performance are still open questions. The docompostion and weak-coupling ideas may provide some insights to this problem: RLHF is a typical example where the reward model and the policy model are weakly coupled, and the overall performance can be improved by smartly updating the two models.
